Physical Design Overview

Physical Design converts a synthesized netlist into a manufacturable layout (GDSII). It determines the placement of millions of standard cells, builds a balanced clock network, routes interconnects, and ensures that timing, power and signal integrity constraints are satisfied. The final output is sent for fabrication where silicon chips are produced.

The goal of physical design is to achieve timing closure, minimize area, maintain power integrity and ensure manufacturability while satisfying all design rule constraints.

Major Physical Design Stages

Floorplanning

Defines the core area, IO placement, macro placement and utilization targets. Good floorplanning reduces congestion and improves timing closure.

Power Planning

Creation of power rings, stripes and grids to distribute VDD and VSS across the chip while minimizing IR drop and electromigration risks.

Placement

Standard cells are placed inside the core region while optimizing timing, wirelength and congestion. Includes legalization and optimization.

Clock Tree Synthesis

Constructs a buffered clock network to distribute clock signals with controlled skew, latency and transition across all sequential elements.

Routing

Connects all nets using metal layers through global and detailed routing while fixing DRC violations and antenna effects.

Static Timing Analysis

Verifies setup and hold timing across all paths under multiple corners and modes ensuring the design meets clock frequency requirements.

Timing Concepts

Setup Timing

Ensures that data launched from a flip-flop arrives at the capture flip-flop before the next clock edge considering clock skew and uncertainty.

Hold Timing

Ensures that data does not change too quickly after the clock edge, preventing incorrect data capture.

Clock Skew

Difference between clock arrival times at launch and capture registers. Controlled during CTS.

OCV & Derates

On-chip variation modeling that accounts for manufacturing variations affecting cell delays and interconnect timing.
